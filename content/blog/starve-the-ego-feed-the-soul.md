There's a version of me from a year ago who would have read that title and scrolled past it. Too soft. Too philosophical. Not productive enough.

That version of me was busy stacking internships, grinding competitions, chasing gold medals, and filling every single gap in my calendar with something that looked good on a CV. Honestly, I was pretty good at it. I won things. I shipped things. From the outside, everything looked like it was going exactly right.

But here's what nobody tells you about running that fast: you stop noticing why you started running in the first place.

I remember one night, sitting in front of my laptop after wrapping up a sprint at work, and I had this strange, hollow feeling. Not sadness, exactly. More like... emptiness. I had just delivered something I was proud of. But the satisfaction lasted maybe ten minutes before my brain was already asking, Okay, what's next? What else can I add?"

That's when it hit me. I wasn't building things because I loved building. I was building things because I loved being _seen_ as someone who builds.

There's a difference that matters more than most of us are willing to admit.

The ego is sneaky like that. It doesn't show up as arrogance. Sometimes it shows up as ambition. As discipline. As "I just have high standards." It disguises itself in all the words we've been taught to admire. And so we feed it without even realizing we're starving something else.

The soul, on the other hand, is quiet. It's the part of you that lights up when you help a student finally understand a concept they've been stuck on for weeks, not because it goes on your resume, but because their face changes. It's the part that finds peace in a slow morning with no agenda. It's the thing that makes you write a blog post at midnight, not for engagement, but because something inside you needed to come out.

I'm not saying ambition is bad. I still care about my career. I still want to build meaningful things, contribute to projects that matter, and grow as an engineer. But I've learned that when ambition becomes the _only_ voice in the room, you start making decisions for a version of yourself that doesn't actually exist yet, while ignoring the one that's here right now.

Life doesn't have to be a highlight reel. It doesn't have to be optimized. Some of the best moments I've had in the past few years were the ones that would never make it onto a CV. A conversation with a friend that went nowhere and everywhere at the same time. A walk with no destination. A book I read slowly, with no intention of "applying" it to anything.

So this is me, trying to learn a new kind of discipline. Not the discipline of doing more, but the discipline of being still. Of letting something be good without needing it to be impressive. Of asking myself, before I say yes to the next thing: _is this feeding my soul, or just my ego?_

I don't have it figured out. I probably won't for a long time. But I think the fact that I'm asking the question at all means something has already shifted.

And maybe that's enough for now.
