There's this pattern I keep noticing when I look back at the last few years. The things that felt like overkill at the time, the late nights, the projects I had no business taking on, the decisions to do things the hard way when easier options were right there. Those are the exact things that got me to where I am now.

I don't say that in a hustle culture, sleep-is-for-the-weak kinda way. Some of it genuinely sucked. But I'm glad I did it, and I think I'd tell my younger self to do it again.

When I was earlier in university, I had this habit of picking the harder option whenever there was a fork in the road. Not because I was trying to be impressive or because someone told me to. It was more like... I knew this was the only time in my life where the cost of failure was low enough to take big swings.

No mortgage. No dependents. No reputation to protect. If I stayed up until 3am trying to understand something and failed, the worst that happened was I'd be tired the next day. That's a pretty cheap price for learning.

I picked up tools and technologies that weren't required for class. I spent hours debugging things that nobody asked me to debug. When a project could be done the simple way or the "proper" way, I almost always chose the proper way, even if it took three times as long.

At the time it felt like I was just making life harder for myself. In hindsight, I was building a foundation I didn't know I'd need.


There's a specific kind of stress that comes from being in over your head on something you're getting paid to deliver. It's different from school stress. In school, if you mess up, you get a bad grade. In contract work, if you mess up, someone's actual product breaks. That pressure forced me to learn faster than any course ever did.

I remember taking on work that involved refactoring codebases I barely understood, working with tools I'd never touched, and deploying to production environments where mistakes had real consequences. Each time, there was a period of "what did I get myself into" followed by hours of grinding through docs and error messages until things clicked.

And then they clicked. Not always gracefully, but they clicked.


I'd be lying if I said there was no downside. There were stretches where I was running on very little sleep, where I skipped things I probably shouldn't have skipped, where I was so locked in on work that I forgot to take care of the basics. There were moments where I burned out and had to just stop for a few days and do nothing.

The thing about going hardcore is that it's not sustainable forever. That's kind of the point. You do it early, when you can absorb the impact, and then you carry the skills and the confidence forward into a phase of life where you hopefully don't have to run at that intensity anymore.

I also want to be honest about the fact that not every late night was productive. Some of that time was wasted spinning my wheels on things I could have asked for help with. Some of it was stubbornness disguised as discipline. Learning to tell the difference between "this is hard and I need to push through" and "this is hard because I'm doing it wrong" took a while. I'm still working on it.



The part that makes it all feel worth it is what happened next. Opportunities started showing up that I could actually say yes to. Not because I got lucky, but because I had already done the hard version of the work that those opportunities required.

When someone needed a mobile developer who could pick up a messy codebase and ship something real, I'd already done that on a smaller scale. When something required learning a new tool quickly under pressure, I'd already been through that cycle enough times that it didn't scare me anymore.

Skills compound in a way that's hard to see while you're building them. You learn one hard thing, and the next hard thing is slightly less hard. Not because it's easier, but because you've been through the process of figuring out something you don't understand, and you trust yourself to do it again.

That trust is the real thing you build when you go hardcore early. Not the specific technical skills (those change), but the confidence that you can sit down with something unfamiliar and come out the other side with it working.


I want to be careful here because this isn't a "just work harder" post. I've seen people burn out permanently by treating every phase of their life like a sprint. That's not what I'm advocating for.

What I am saying is that there's a window, usually in university or the first few years of your career, where the conditions for going hard are uniquely good. Low obligations, high tolerance for failure, and a brain that's wired to absorb new things quickly. That window doesn't stay open forever.

I used mine. Not perfectly. Not without cost. But I used it.

And now, when I look at the work I'm able to do and the opportunities I'm able to take, I can trace almost all of it back to that period where I chose the harder path when I didn't have to.

That's the bet. Go hardcore early.
